Every major technology shift produces a defining product problem that the generation before it did not have to solve. For mobile it was designing for touch and intermittent connectivity. For cloud it was building for distributed failure and infinite scale. For AI it is verification.

The generation problem — getting AI to produce useful output — is largely solved for most use cases. The models are good. The tooling is accessible. Anyone can get an AI system generating something plausible in an afternoon.

The verification problem — knowing whether that output can be trusted, and building products that make the answer visible — is almost entirely unsolved at the product layer. The research is there. The $67.4 billion in annual hallucination costs is there. The 47% of executives making decisions on unverified AI output is there. The structural proof that hallucinations cannot be engineered away is there.

What is not there yet is a product management discipline built around verification as a first-class requirement. Most PM frameworks were designed for a world where the system either works or it does not. AI introduces a third state: the system produces output that is wrong in ways that look right. That state requires a different design approach than anything the field has developed before.

I think the PMs who define that approach — who build the frameworks, the patterns, the tools for making AI output trustworthy rather than just impressive — are going to matter more than the PMs who ship the most AI features the fastest.

Building fast is a skill. Building something people can actually rely on is a harder one. The industry has not fully separated those two things yet. When it does, the work that matters will look a lot less like generation and a lot more like verification.
