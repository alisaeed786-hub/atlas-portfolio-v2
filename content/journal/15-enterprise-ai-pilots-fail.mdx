MIT research found that 95% of enterprise AI pilots fail to produce measurable return on investment. That number gets cited constantly as evidence that AI is overhyped or that companies are moving too fast.

I think that reading is wrong.

The pilots are not failing because the technology does not work. They are failing because companies are deploying AI without defining what correct output looks like. They pick a use case, run a demo that impresses someone in a conference room, ship it, and six months later the output is inconsistent, users have stopped trusting it, and nobody can explain why.

Not because the model was bad. Because nobody did the product work before the deployment. Nobody asked: what does a correct answer look like in this context? What happens when the system is wrong? How do users know the difference? Those are not engineering questions. They are product questions. And they are being skipped.

The companies getting real ROI from AI are treating it like any other critical system. They define correctness criteria up front. They build verification into the workflow. They make failure visible rather than hiding it behind confident-sounding output. That discipline does not require a bigger AI budget. It requires a PM who understands that shipping an AI feature and shipping a trustworthy AI product are two different things.

My take: the 95% failure rate is not an AI problem. It is a product management problem. And it will not improve until more teams treat defining what correct looks like as the first task, not the last.
