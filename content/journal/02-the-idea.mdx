The idea for ProductProof started with a ticket.

Not a specific ticket — I cannot even remember which one it was. But I remember the feeling. It was in refinement, and I was reading through the acceptance criteria, and I had this moment where I realized that at least two of the items on the list were assumptions that nobody had actually verified against our documentation. They were written as facts. They were not facts.

I brought it up. We spent twenty minutes tracking down whether our system actually supported what the ticket claimed it supported. It did not. The ticket had to be rewritten.

That was not unusual. That was Tuesday.

The thing I kept thinking about was not the time it took to catch it. It was the time it would have taken if we had not. If that ticket had gone into a sprint unreviewed, an engineer would have started building against an assumption, discovered the gap mid-sprint, and we would have spent three times as long untangling it.

I started wondering whether that process — reading a ticket, checking its claims against your own documentation, flagging what is verified versus assumed — was something a system could do faster than a human.

I almost did not build it because I was not sure it was a real problem or just a Tuesday problem. The thing that convinced me was realizing I could not remember a single refinement session where this had not come up in some form.

That seemed like enough of a signal to build something.
