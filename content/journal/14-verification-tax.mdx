Microsoft's 2025 data found that knowledge workers spend an average of 4.3 hours per week verifying AI output. That is more than half a workday, every single week, just checking whether the AI was right.

For a 500-person organization, that works out to roughly $7.1 million per year spent entirely on verification. Not on building. Not on shipping. On checking homework.

And Deloitte found that 47% of enterprise executives have made at least one major business decision based on hallucinated AI content. Not junior analysts. Executives. Making major decisions. On things that were not true.

Here is what I find strange about this. The product management community spends enormous energy on generation — better prompts, better models, better AI features. Almost nobody treats verification as a first-class design problem. The assumption seems to be that better models will eventually solve the hallucination problem and verification will take care of itself.

That assumption is wrong. MIT research confirmed in 2025 that hallucinations cannot be fully eliminated from current large language model architectures through any combination of training improvements or fact-checking mechanisms. They are structural. They are not going away.

So the 4.3 hours per week is not a temporary tax that disappears when the models improve. It is the cost of using AI without a verification layer built into the product. And right now most products are shipping without one.

ProductProof exists because I ran into this problem directly. Tickets generated with AI assistance that sounded correct and were not. The tool I built is one answer to one version of this problem. The broader answer — building verification into AI products as a first-class requirement rather than an afterthought — is something the whole industry needs to figure out.
