Two years ago being a good PM meant writing clear requirements, running effective sprint ceremonies, and keeping stakeholders aligned. Those things still matter. They are just not the job anymore.

The job now is understanding how AI systems actually work well enough to make good decisions about them. Not at the level of a researcher or an engineer. But well enough to know when a model is likely to be wrong, what inputs are going to produce unreliable outputs, and where the verification layer needs to go. That is a fundamentally different skill than anything the PM role required before.

Productboard found that 59% of product professionals believe strategy and business acumen are the most important skills for the next two to three years. I agree with half of that. Strategy matters. But strategy without technical credibility is just a slide deck. If you cannot sit in a room with engineers and ask the right questions about how the system is actually going to behave, your strategy is being built on assumptions you cannot see.

The PMs who are doing the most interesting work right now are the ones who treated AI as something to understand rather than something to manage. They are the ones who built something, watched it break, figured out why, and built it again. That cycle teaches you things no framework can.

My take: the new PM job description has not been officially written yet. But the PMs who write it for themselves before their companies define it for them are going to be the ones in the rooms where the interesting decisions get made.
